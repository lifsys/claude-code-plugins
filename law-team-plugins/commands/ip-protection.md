Provide comprehensive guidance on protecting intellectual property assets.

## Instructions

1. Identify the type of intellectual property to protect (from arguments or ask):
   - Trademark (brand names, logos, slogans)
   - Patent (inventions, processes, designs)
   - Copyright (creative works, software, content)
   - Trade secret (confidential business information)
   - All/general assessment

2. For each IP type, provide:

   **Trademarks:**
   - What can be trademarked: brand names, logos, slogans, colors, sounds
   - Search requirements: USPTO TESS database check
   - Federal vs. state registration
   - Classes of goods/services
   - Application process and timeline (6-12 months)
   - Costs: $250-$350 per class (government fees)
   - Use requirements: "TM" vs. "Â®"
   - Maintenance: renewals, continued use

   **Patents:**
   - Types: Utility, design, plant patents
   - Requirements: novel, non-obvious, useful
   - Prior art search importance
   - Provisional vs. non-provisional applications
   - Patent pending status
   - Timeline: 1-3 years typically
   - Costs: $5,000-$15,000+ (with attorney)
   - International protection: PCT applications
   - Maintenance fees

   **Copyrights:**
   - Automatic protection upon creation
   - Registration benefits: statutory damages, attorney's fees
   - Application process: online through copyright.gov
   - Costs: $45-$65 per work
   - Timeline: 3-6 months for registration
   - Duration: Life + 70 years (or 95/120 for corporate works)
   - Fair use considerations
   - DMCA protections

   **Trade Secrets:**
   - Definition: confidential business information providing competitive advantage
   - Examples: formulas, processes, customer lists, algorithms
   - Protection measures:
     - Non-disclosure agreements (NDAs)
     - Confidentiality clauses in employment agreements
     - Physical/digital security measures
     - Access controls
     - Employee training
   - No registration or fees
   - Duration: indefinite if maintained
   - Risks: reverse engineering, independent discovery

3. Provide a protection strategy:
   - Immediate steps to take
   - Short-term goals (1-3 months)
   - Long-term IP strategy
   - Budget considerations
   - International protection if relevant

4. Create a timeline with deadlines and critical dates

5. Recommend whether to DIY or hire an IP attorney

6. Provide resources:
   - USPTO.gov (trademarks and patents)
   - Copyright.gov (copyrights)
   - State trademark offices
   - IP attorney referrals
